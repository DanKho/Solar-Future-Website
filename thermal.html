Did you know?

Solar thermal energy is primarily used domestically for space heating, hot water, and in some cases air conditioning. Solar thermal energy is renewable and no fuels are required during the process to generate electricity.

What is thermal energy and how can it be used?

Solar energy is not always used to generate electricity. In some scenarios, it can be converted into thermal (heat) energy which has a variety of unique uses on its own. Among the most common devices used to capture solar energy and convert it to thermal energy are flat-plate collectors, which are used for solar heating applications. The most widely used flat-plate collectors consist of a blackened metal plate, covered with one or two sheets of glass, that is heated by the sunlight falling on it. This heat is then transferred to air or water, called carrier fluids, that flow past the back of the plate. The heat may be used directly, or it may be transferred to another medium for storage. Flat-plate collectors are commonly used for solar water heaters and house heating. Such a system can supply a home with hot water drawn from the storage tank, or, with the warmed water flowing through tubes in floors and ceilings, it can provide space heating.

Benefits of solar heating:

Using a solar water heating system has a range of advantages over the traditional electrical and gas-based solutions. The first and most lucrative benefit is reduced energy bills. Sunlight is free, so once you've paid for the initial installation fees, your hot water costs will be significantly reduced. The second advantage is flexibility. Solar heating can be easily integrated into the existing water heating systems, thereby making your hot water supply fully independent from the weather conditions (e.g. in winter). The third advantage is sustainability. Solar heating systems produce zero net emission of greenhouse gases, and can significantly reduce our consumption of fossil fuels.

Limitations of solar heating:

Despite the advantages of solar heating systems, there are still factors to consider when deciding to switch to solar. As with the benefits, most of the drawbacks of solar thermal overlap with those of solar energy. The first disadvantage is its cost. Like solar panels, the installation of solar hot water systems is relatively expensive and should be viewed as a serious long-term investment. The second drawback is the efficiency. It is obvious that there will be less solar energy available in winter, which correlates with the availability of hot water during the colder months. Another important consideration is the space usage. This is especially important when integrating a solar heating system with an existing one.
